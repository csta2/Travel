Pros

  • Only 2 luggage moves total (arrival + departure) — lowest disruption risk of either plan
  • Every night, including the wedding night, is in the same bed
  • Covers Puglia’s most iconic inland sights: the UNESCO-listed trulli of Alberobello (1996), Lecce’s baroque core, and Ostuni’s hilltop old town
  • Valle d’Itria is where trulli architecture is most concentrated and best preserved anywhere in Puglia

Cons

  • Longest total transit time of the two plans (~10h 22m), since Valle d’Itria requires routing via Bari
  • Alberobello is Puglia’s most touristic stop — expect cruise-excursion crowds even in the UNESCO district
  • Coastal swimming near Monopoli is mostly small rocky coves, not Salento’s sandy beaches
  • FSE service to Alberobello thins out on Sundays, so day-of-week matters for that leg

Salento vs Valle d’Itria

The two options above aren’t just different logistics — they represent two distinct regions of Puglia with different histories, landscapes, and protected sites.

Valle d’Itria

Ostuni, Alberobello, Locorotondo

History & recognition

  • Alberobello’s trulli district: UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1996, recognized as an outstanding example of dry-stone vernacular architecture still inhabited today
  • Locorotondo is an officially recognized "borgo più bello d’Italia" (one of Italy’s most beautiful villages)
  • Ostuni’s old town traces to Messapian settlement, later Norman-fortified; the region has been inhabited since the Stone Age

Nature

  • Torre Guaceto Marine Protected Area near Ostuni/Carovigno: ~2,200 ha, WWF-managed since 1991, nesting site for loggerhead sea turtles
  • Coastal access from the valley is mostly small rocky coves rather than long sandy beaches

Trade-off

  • Alberobello is Puglia’s single most touristic stop — cruise-excursion crowds are a real factor even on a weekday
  • Inland location means no direct beach access from the towns themselves

Salento

Otranto, Gallipoli

History & recognition

  • Otranto Cathedral holds one of the only complete Norman-era mosaic floors surviving in Italy (1163–65), plus the Chapel of the Martyrs: relics of 813 residents killed in the 1480 Ottoman siege, canonized by Pope Francis in 2013
  • Gallipoli’s centro storico occupies its own small limestone island, with an Aragonese castle and a fishing tradition still active today

Nature

  • Porto Selvaggio Regional Natural Park near Gallipoli/Nardò: ~1,000–1,100 ha of pine forest, cliffs and sea caves, protected by regional law since 2006
  • Real sandy swimming beaches at Baia dei Turchi (Otranto) and La Purità (Gallipoli), plus long Ionian-coast stretches further south

Trade-off

  • No trulli, no UNESCO architecture on this side of the region
  • Public transport (especially to Otranto) is more seasonal and thins outside summer
